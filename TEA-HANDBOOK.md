# The SlowCup Tea Handbook

*A learner's guide to how tea works — one plant, six families, and how to read a tea's name.*

Everything here has been fact-checked against independent, non-vendor sources. Where the tea world genuinely
disagrees or a claim is folklore, it says so. This is the human-readable companion to the reference data that
powers SlowCup's "tell me about this tea" feature — but you can read it start to finish on its own.

---

## 1. The one idea that explains everything

**All true tea is the same plant: *Camellia sinensis*.** Green, white, oolong, black, yellow, pu-erh — every
one of them starts as a leaf from the same species. There is no "black tea plant" or "green tea bush." What
makes a green tea green and a black tea black is *what people do to the leaf after they pick it.*

Two variables do most of the work:

- **Oxidation** — when you bruise or crush a tea leaf and let it sit in air, enzymes turn it darker and change
  its flavour (the same browning you see in a cut apple). Tea makers *control* this: stop it early and you get
  green; let it run to completion and you get black; stop it partway and you get oolong.
- **"Kill-green" (fixing)** — heating the leaf (by pan-firing or steaming) *destroys* those enzymes and locks
  oxidation wherever it currently is. When and whether you do this is the other master switch.

Hold those two ideas and the whole map falls into place:

| Family | Oxidation | The defining move |
|---|---|---|
| Green | ~0% | Kill-green *early* (pan-fire or steam) to keep it fresh and grassy |
| White | very low | Barely touched — just withered and dried |
| Yellow | low | Like green, plus a slow "sealed yellowing" step |
| Oolong | ~10–80% | Partially oxidised, then kill-green, usually rolled and often roasted |
| Black | ~100% | Fully oxidised, *no* kill-green |
| Dark (pu-erh) | varies | *Fermented by microbes* after the leaf is already made |

That last row is the odd one out and we'll come back to it. Everything else is a story about how far you let
oxidation run before you stop it.

## 2. The verbs of tea (processing, in plain language)

Tea names sound exotic, but the *making* is a short list of physical actions. Learn these ten verbs and you can
follow almost any tea's story.

- **Plucking** — which leaves you take. "One bud and two leaves" is the classic standard; bud-only is the most
  delicate and expensive; older/lower leaves make coarser tea.
- **Withering** — letting the fresh leaf wilt and lose water, so it's soft enough to work and starts developing
  aroma.
- **Kill-green (*sha qing* 杀青)** — heating the leaf to stop oxidation. Two ways: **pan-firing** (the Chinese
  default) or **steaming** (the Japanese default). This is the on/off switch for the "green" character.
- **Rolling / shaping** — bruising and twisting the leaf. This breaks cell walls (helping oxidation and
  releasing juices) and gives the leaf its final shape — needles, strips, balls, pearls.
- **Oxidation** — the waiting game where enzymes darken the leaf. In oolong and black tea this is where most of
  the flavour is born.
- **The "making green" shake (*zuo qing* / *yao qing*)** — for oolong specifically: repeatedly shaking and
  bruising the leaf edges to coax out aroma while the middle stays green. (See §5 — this is *every* oolong's
  step, not one type's secret.)
- **Roasting (firing)** — applying heat after the tea is basically made, to dry it further and add toasty,
  caramel, mineral depth. Traditional charcoal roasting is called **tan bei (炭焙)**.
- **Shading** — growing the plant under cover for weeks before harvest (a Japanese speciality). Starves the leaf
  of light, which pushes up sweetness and umami and drops bitterness. Gives gyokuro and matcha their intensity.
- **Wet-piling (*wo dui* 渥堆)** — heaping damp leaf so *microbes* ferment it. This is the one step unique to
  ripe pu-erh; nothing else in tea does it.
- **Drying / compression** — the finish. Most tea is dried by hot air; some (pu-erh, some Yunnan black) is
  **sun-dried**, which leaves it able to age. Some is then **steamed soft and pressed into cakes** for storage
  and aging.

Almost every tea in the world is just a different *ordering and emphasis* of these ten actions.

## 3. The six families

### Green — *stop it before it starts*

Kill-green comes early, so the leaf stays close to fresh. Little to no oxidation. The big split is **how** you
kill-green:

- **Pan-fired (Chinese default)** — nuttier, toastier, rounder. Longjing (Dragon Well), Biluochun, Mao Feng,
  Gunpowder.
- **Steamed (Japanese default)** — greener, grassier, more umami and marine. Sencha, gyokuro, matcha.

Two axes shape Japanese greens especially (SlowCup models these as *attributes*, not separate teas):

- **Shading**: unshaded → *kabuse* (half, ~1–2 weeks) → *gyokuro* (full, 3–4 weeks). More shade = sweeter,
  more umami, less bitter.
- **Steaming depth**: *asamushi* (light) → *chumushi* (medium) → *fukamushi* (deep). Deeper steaming breaks the
  leaf up more, so it brews faster and greener — **shorten your steeps for fukamushi.**

*Naming trap:* **Anji Bai Cha** ("Anji white tea") is a **green tea**, not white — the "white" refers to a
pale, low-chlorophyll cultivar, not the processing. Never trust "white" in a name alone.

*Brew:* cool water. 70–80 °C for most; gyokuro as low as 50–60 °C; short steeps.

### White — *barely do anything*

The least-processed family. Ideally just **withered and dried** — no kill-green, no rolling, minimal oxidation.
Delicate, sweet, hay-and-honey. Chinese white is graded by how much bud vs leaf:

- **Bai Hao Yin Zhen (Silver Needle)** — bud only; lightest, sweetest.
- **Bai Mu Dan (White Peony)** — one bud + one or two leaves; fuller.
- **Gong Mei** — one bud + two or three leaves.
- **Shou Mei** — one bud + three or four leaves; boldest, few visible buds.

*Loose vs cake is a real processing fork:* pressing white into a cake adds two steps — steaming to soften, then
compression — and makes it age better. **Yue Guang Bai (Moonlight White)** is a distinct Yunnan white with
striking two-tone dark/light leaves.

*Brew:* 80–85 °C; never boiling. Silver Needle wants a short first steep (30–40 s).

### Yellow — *green tea's rare, patient cousin*

Made almost like a green tea, but with one extra slow step: **men huang (闷黄, "sealed yellowing")** — the warm,
damp leaf is wrapped and left to mellow, softening the grassy edge into something rounder and sweeter. It's
labour-intensive and often skipped (so a lot of "yellow" tea on the market is really green). Huang Ya
("yellow buds") and Junshan Yinzhen are the classics. Men huang is genuinely unique to yellow tea — a real
class fingerprint.

*Brew:* like a gentle green, ~80 °C.

### Oolong — *the huge middle ground*

The most varied family, because "partial oxidation" spans a vast range (~10% to ~80%) and oolongs are usually
**rolled and often roasted** on top. Two big shape styles:

- **Ball-rolled** — tight little nuggets that unfurl over many steeps. Tie Guan Yin, most Taiwanese
  high-mountain (gaoshan), Dong Ding, milk oolong.
- **Strip / twisted** — long open leaves. Wuyi rock teas, Phoenix Dan Cong, Oriental Beauty.

The oxidation ladder inside oolong, low to high: **Baozhong** (~10–20%, almost green) → high-mountain gaoshan →
Tie Guan Yin / Dong Ding → **Wuyi rock tea** (~40–70%) → **Dan Cong / Oriental Beauty** (~50–80%) → "hong/black
oolong" (pushed nearly to black). See §5 for the two famous roasted oolongs people always mix up.

*Oolong is a process, not a place.* It's most associated with Fujian, Guangdong and Taiwan, but it's genuinely
made in Japan, Nepal, Thailand and Java too. "Oolong" tells you the processing, not the country.

*Brew:* near-boiling (95–100 °C), gongfu style — lots of leaf, short repeated steeps.

### Black (*hong cha* 红茶) — *let it go all the way*

**Fully oxidised, no kill-green.** Wither → roll → oxidise completely → dry. Bold, malty, sweet. In China it's
called **"red tea" (hong cha)** for the colour of the liquor — which sets up the single biggest naming trap in
tea (see §6). The Chinese classics:

- **Dian Hong** — Yunnan, big-leafed, golden-tipped, malty and cocoa-ish.
- **Keemun (Qimen)** — Anhui; wine, cocoa, orchid; the backbone of English Breakfast.
- **Lapsang Souchong (Zheng Shan Xiao Zhong)** — Tongmu in Wuyi; **the original black tea** (17th century);
  traditionally pine-smoked, though unsmoked versions are now common.
- **Jin Jun Mei ("Golden Eyebrow")** — from the same area, but a **modern (2005), premium, all-golden-bud** tea;
  honeyed, unsmoked, and heavily faked, so buy carefully.

Black tea is the one family where China is a *minority* of world output — Assam, Darjeeling, Ceylon, Kenya and
Nepal are all black-tea giants, and they're siblings under the same family.

*Brew:* Chinese gongfu blacks like ~85–95 °C (too hot turns them bitter); Western style ~90–95 °C for 3–4 min.

### Dark (*hei cha* 黑茶) — *let microbes finish the job*

The genuinely different family: **post-fermented**, meaning living microbes transform the leaf *after* it's
already been made into tea. **Pu-erh** from Yunnan is the famous member (others exist: Liu Bao from Guangxi,
Anhua dark tea from Hunan). Pu-erh comes in two kinds:

- **Sheng (raw)** — sun-dried loose leaf ("mao cha"), pressed into cakes, then **aged slowly over years to
  decades** by natural fermentation. Young sheng is green, brisk, even bitter; aged sheng turns mellow, woody,
  honeyed. Crucially, **it is not a green tea** — the gentle kill-green and sun-drying deliberately leave it
  alive to age.
- **Shou (ripe / "cooked")** — the leaf is **wet-piled (wo dui)** for a few weeks to force-ferment it, an
  accelerated method invented in 1973. Dark, smooth, earthy, drinkable immediately.

Beyond raw-vs-ripe, everything is an *attribute*: the shape it's pressed into (cake, tuocha bowl, brick, tiny
"dragon ball"), the mountain it came from (Yiwu, Bingdao, Bulang…), the vintage, and the factory (Dayi,
Xiaguan) with its cryptic recipe numbers.

*Brew:* boiling or near-boiling; **rinse the leaves once first**, then many short steeps.

## 4. The cross-cutting attributes (the real mental model)

Here's the insight that took this project a while to earn, and it's the thing most tea guides miss: **many of
the words on a tea label describe *techniques that cut across families*, not the family itself.** A tea is
better understood as *a family plus a handful of independent dials* than as one name on a flat list.

The dials:

- **Roast** — its *method* (charcoal / *tan bei* vs electric oven) and its *level* (light "qing xiang" → medium
  → heavy). Applies to Wuyi, Taiwanese, Anxi and Dan Cong oolongs alike.
- **Oxidation** — a continuum, clearest inside oolong, but it's what separates the families too.
- **Shading** and **steaming depth** — the two Japanese-green dials from §3.
- **Harvest / season** — *shincha* (first-flush Japanese green) and *dong pian* (a rare late-winter Taiwanese
  oolong pluck) are the *same tea* picked at a special time, not new types.
- **Bug-bitten** — letting tiny leafhoppers nibble the leaves triggers a honey-muscatel aroma. Famous in
  Oriental Beauty, but also Gui Fei, some Dong Ding, and Taiwan's Ruby (Hong Yu) black.
- **Smoked** — pine-smoke over the leaf (traditional Lapsang Souchong). An option, not a definition.
- **Drying** — hot-air vs **sun-dried (shai)**; sun-dried teas can age.
- **Compression** — pressed into cakes/bricks; adds a steam-and-press step; enables aging.
- **Flavoured / scented** — jasmine white, lychee black, ginseng oolong, the tangerine-stuffed pu-erh "xiao
  qing gan," and most supermarket "milk oolong." A real category, but it's an *added* thing, not a processing
  family.
- **Terroir & vintage** — where and when. Decisive for Wuyi ("rock rhyme" from cliff soil) and pu-erh (mountain
  + year).

When you read a label, you're really reading: *[family] + [some of these dials]*. That's the whole game.

## 5. Three pairs people always confuse

### Wuyi rock tea vs Phoenix Dan Cong

Both are Chinese, strip-shaped, roasted, partially-oxidised oolongs — so they look like twins. They're built
around different heroes:

- **Rock tea (Wuyi yancha) is about PLACE.** The goal is *yan yun* — "rock rhyme," a mineral signature from the
  cliffs of Wuyishan. It's an appellation tea. Its hero step is **charcoal roasting**, done in repeated rounds
  over weeks; the roast *creates* the deep, mineral, caramelised character.
- **Dan Cong is about the CULTIVAR'S AROMA.** The goal is to isolate one single bush's natural fragrance —
  honey-orchid, almond, gardenia, even the famous "duck-shit" aroma. Its hero step is the **overnight shaking
  ("making green")** that develops those aromatics; the roast is lighter and *serves* the aroma rather than
  defining it.

In one line: **rock tea's roast makes the flavour; Dan Cong's roast protects a flavour the leaf already has.**
(Watch out: "Rou Gui" and "Shui Xian" each name *two different teas* — a Wuyi cultivar *and* a Dan Cong aroma.)

### Milk oolong: natural vs faked

Yes — **Jin Xuan** (金萱, "Golden Lily," Taiwan cultivar #12) is a real cultivar whose creaminess is
*genuinely natural*, coming from aromatic compounds (lactones) plus a thick, coating mouthfeel. But most "milk
oolong" sold — especially in the West — is **cheap tea sprayed with milk flavouring**. Tells: a strong
candy/condensed-milk smell *before* you brew, and a creaminess that vanishes after the first steep. The
"steamed over milk" origin story is a myth. Label tell: authentic lists only "oolong / Jin Xuan"; flavoured
lists "…flavouring."

### Sheng vs shou pu-erh

Same leaf, two philosophies. **Sheng** ages *naturally and slowly* — you're laying it down like wine. **Shou**
is *force-fermented* by wet-piling so it's dark and smooth right away. Young sheng can be sharp and green; shou
is earthy and forgiving from day one.

## 6. How to read a tea's name (the part no one teaches)

Tea names are not tidy. A single name can be *place + processing + cultivar + poetry*, in any combination, and
the pieces don't compose reliably. This is the single most useful skill, and it's mostly about knowing the
traps.

**What a name can be made of:**
- a **place** (Anxi, Wuyi, Yiwu, Uji, Darjeeling),
- a **style/processing** (Dan Cong = "single bush," Gongfu/Congou = fine strip black, Baozhong),
- a **cultivar** (Jin Xuan, Rou Gui, Qing Xin, Tie Guan Yin),
- a **grade or pluck** (Silver Needle = bud-only; Shou Mei = coarser),
- or pure **poetry/marketing** ("Iron Goddess of Mercy," "Big Red Robe," "Golden Eyebrow," "Moonlight White").

**The traps that will actually catch you:**

1. **"Black" means two different families depending on language.** English **"black tea" = Chinese hong cha
   (红, "red")** — fully oxidised, like Keemun. Chinese **hei cha (黑, "black") = the dark/post-fermented family
   = pu-erh.** Same word, opposite ends of the shelf.
2. **"White" in a name isn't always white tea.** *Anji Bai Cha* is a green tea (albino cultivar). *Yue Guang
   Bai* is a real white. *Jasmine Silver Needle* is a real white that's been *scented*.
3. **A shared technique isn't a class marker.** *Tan bei* (charcoal roasting) shows up on Wuyi rock tea *and*
   Taiwanese Dong Ding *and* Dan Cong — it's a method, not a "this is a Wuyi tea" signal.
4. **The same name can be two teas.** Wuyi "Rou Gui" (a cinnamon-aroma rock-tea cultivar) vs Dan Cong "Rou Gui
   Xiang" (a cinnamon *aroma* type). Wuyi "Shui Xian" vs Phoenix "Shui Xian." Context decides.
5. **Numbers can be recipe codes, not years.** A pu-erh "7542" isn't a date or a weight — it's a factory recipe
   code: [recipe year][leaf grade][factory].
6. **"Souchong" = xiao zhong** (a leaf grade / small-leaf), and not all Lapsang is smoky.
7. **"Milk / creamy / ginseng / lychee" usually means flavoured** — an added ingredient, not a tea type.

The healthy habit: treat a name as a *set of clues to confirm*, never as a fact. The leaf's **shape** is fairly
reliable from the name; its **roast and oxidation** are the maker's choices and should be checked, not assumed.

## 7. Quick reference

**Brewing temperature, rough guide:**

| Family | Water temp | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Green (Japanese) | 60–75 °C | Gyokuro lowest; deep-steamed brews fast |
| Green (Chinese) | 75–85 °C | |
| White | 80–85 °C | Never boiling |
| Yellow | ~80 °C | Gentle, like green |
| Oolong | 95–100 °C | Gongfu: lots of leaf, short steeps |
| Black (Chinese) | 85–95 °C | Too hot = bitter |
| Pu-erh | 95–100 °C | Rinse first, then many steeps |

**The oxidation ladder, whole spectrum:**

White (barely) → Green (~0, but *fixed*, not oxidised) → Yellow (low) → Baozhong (10–20%) → Tie Guan Yin /
Dong Ding (~30–50%) → Wuyi rock (40–70%) → Dan Cong / Oriental Beauty (50–80%) → Black (~100%). *Pu-erh sits
off this line entirely — it's fermented, not oxidised.*

## 8. Where to go deeper

The natural next steps, once the six families feel solid: taste **one contrast pair back-to-back** (an unshaded
sencha next to a gyokuro; a green Tie Guan Yin next to a roasted one; a young sheng next to a shou). Nothing
teaches the model faster than putting two cups side by side and feeling the single variable move between them.

*This handbook is a living document — it grows as new teas and traps are verified. If a claim here ever
contradicts what a trusted vendor or a tea scientist tells you, believe the evidence and flag it; that's how
the reference stays honest.*
